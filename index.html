Agile is supposed to make our processes and collaboration efforts more efficient. So why does it feel like it’s holding us back?

Agile is now more than 20 years old. It’s a development process, not a design process.

UX has never been a consideration in implementing Agile processes. Nothing is mentioned about delivering great experiences in the original Agile Values or Manifesto documents. Agile was always about making development more efficient.

Unfortunately, conventional thinking about Agile doesn’t help us with this. Agile was not conceived with UX in mind — we’re on our own to make this work.

It’s time to Take control of Agile UX.

The way we’re working has matured. Our developers are now ready to iterate quickly and work more flexibly. Product sees research as more and more valuable. Project management now includes UX into deeper levels of strategic thinking.

Yet we’re still treating Agile the same.

We must adapt our Agile process to not hold us back. We need an Agile UX process that delivers innovative designs to our customers and users.

It’s time to take what we’ve learned over the past 20 years and piece together a modern, redesigned approach to Agile UX.

Monday, October 16:
Reframe Agile to Deliver Great UX

90 minutes at either 12pm or 7pm ET (16.00 or 23.00 GMT)

We must change our approach to Agile and to UX to have the impact we want.

Much of what holds us back is outdated thinking about UX and Agile. We’re stuck in 2001 when Agile was conceived, and today’s UX practices were in their infancy. We’ve learned so much in the last two decades. We know how to do better.

Agile and UX can work together. To do that, we need to reframe some misconceptions about how we put them together. We need to replace old, dysfunctional habits with state-of-the-art techniques and processes.

At Monday’s session, uncover how your UX team will:

  • Improve your Agile teams’ delivery capabilities by building a shared understanding of your users’ needs.
  • Influence the products your teams deliver as you shift from reactive to proactive UX efforts.
  • Stop feeling trapped and frustrated by short sprints. Not your UX work will continually contribute value from concept through delivery.

Tuesday, October 17:
Use UX Research to Drive the Meaning of “Done”

90 minutes at either 12pm or 7pm ET (16.00 or 23.00 GMT)

Continuous research is the best way to grow the influence of UX work.

The 'definition of done' is at the center of successful Agile implementations.’ This definition is an agreement amongst team members as to when they should stop working on one piece and move on to others. The definition of done is where UX leaders can strongly influence what the teams deliver as products and services.

For Agile and UX to successfully work together, the definition of done must have the users’ needs with all their subtlety and nuance baked in. We must infuse the definition of done with extensive user research to drive a more human-centered product development process.

At Tuesday’s session, discover how your UX team will:

  • Focus your Agile teams on improving your users' lives by determining what done looks like from comprehensive UX research.
  • Solve important (and valuable) problems for users and customers by developing a rich, shared understanding of your users’ needs and challenges.
  • Contribute real value to every critical product decision as your team grows a continuous research effort essential to the organization’s success.

Wednesday, October 18:
Establish UX Metrics for Agile

90 minutes at either 12pm or 7pm ET (16.00 or 23.00 GMT)

What gets measured gets done. Better UX measures drive better UX.

We need to measure success. We need to measure progress. Most importantly, we need those measurements to be completely UX-driven.

While business outcomes are important, we only truly succeed when everyone focuses on building products and services that improve people’s lives. We must identify and monitor effective UX metrics to track our progress and celebrate our successes.

At Wednesday’s session, explore how your UX team will:

  • Demonstrate key wins when your Agile teams deliver products that improve your users’ lives because you transformed the teams’ KPIs and OKRs to be human-centered.
  • Showcase to your executives and key stakeholders when your teams deliver the best-designed products, showing how UX contributes value to your organization’s most critical objectives.
  • Uncover ongoing organizational costs due to continually delivering poor UX, saving millions when you eliminate those costs.

Thursday, October 19:
Build Your Agile Team’s UX Capabilities

90 minutes at either 12pm or 7pm ET (16.00 or 23.00 GMT)

Great UX happens when the right people make smart decisions.

Agile only succeeds when teams work autonomously. In every sprint, an Agile team faces dozens (if not hundreds) of critical decisions affecting the users’ eventual experience. We will never have enough UX team members in the room to make all of those critical decisions. How do we ensure every Agile team member makes high-quality design decisions?

We must increase each Agile team member’s UX skills and responsibilities. Our UX leaders must shift from thinking it’s their responsibility to make those UX decisions. Instead we can lead every team into making smart decisions that result in substantially improved products and services.

At Thursday’s session, discover how your UX team will:

  • Grow into influential organizational leaders that empower and enable great design across a comprehensive portfolio of products and services.
  • Raise your organization’s overall UX maturity as Agile teams now see why delivering well-designed products is essential.
  • Create a culture of continuous learning, not just about UX, but about how great UX makes your organization seen as visionary when you raise an Agile team’s UX literacy and fluency.

Friday, October 20:
Inspire Your Agile Teams with What Users Truly Need

90 minutes at either 12pm or 7pm ET (16.00 or 23.00 GMT)

Paint a picture of product success that has great UX as the centerpiece.

If we don’t deliver, we don’t succeed. Yet, if we don’t deliver what users need, what we deliver doesn’t matter.

We can’t afford to miss customer expectations. We need to make sure we’re not overcomplicating our products or services. Everything we deliver needs to exceed our users’ and customers’ expectations. We must build a shared understanding of our users’ needs into our Agile process’s basic framework.

At Friday’s session, uncover how your UX team will:

  • Inspire your Agile team members to deliver the best-designed products and services because you created a strong connection between team members and their users.
  • Guide your product leadership to prioritize UX debt as they see how any remaining debt cancels out the positive value of their product.
  • Deliver products and services that delight your customers and users because your Agile teams have eliminated frustration and built a great user experience.
